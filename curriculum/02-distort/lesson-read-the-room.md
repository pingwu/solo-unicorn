# Lesson: Read the Room

**Patterns People Can't See in Themselves**

*Novel Reference: "Just Ask" by Pinghsien Wu, Chapter 4*

---

## Opening Excerpt

> Chen Wei-Lun was compact and still. Charcoal blazer over a black shirt, dark trousers. Everything intentional. Dark eyes that seemed to hold contact with everyone simultaneously. He sat on a stool, adjusted the microphone, and let thirty seconds of silence do his work for him.
>
> "You're already doing it," he said.
>
> "When you walked in, you chose a seat. You didn't think about it. But you made that choice based on pattern recognition that took your unconscious mind about four hundred milliseconds. Proximity to exits. Distance from the stage. Whether the person next to you was someone you wanted to be near."

*From "Just Ask" by Pinghsien Wu, Chapter 4*

Chen -- a clinical psychologist turned Ericksonian hypnosis instructor -- reads people the way David reads system logs: by noticing what is absent, not just what is present.

---

## The Concept: Seeing What Others Delete

Chen demonstrates a principle that changes how David sees himself and his future audience: people communicate constantly through what they leave out.

> "I don't know what to look *for*," he said. "I know what to look *at*. The difference matters."

*From "Just Ask" by Pinghsien Wu, Chapter 4*

This is the skill your landing page needs. Your audience does not know what they need -- not precisely. They know they have a problem. They have tried to solve it. They have failed. But they cannot articulate what is missing because the thing that is missing is invisible to them. It has been deleted from their awareness.

Your job is to be Chen: to see the pattern they cannot see in themselves, and to reflect it back in language that makes them say, "That is exactly my problem."

Chen also teaches David about the power of metaphor as the operating language of the unconscious:

> "Your mind does not distinguish between vivid imagination and memory. Neurologically, the same regions activate. So when you vividly imagine a future state -- when you see it, feel it, inhabit it -- your unconscious mind begins treating it as real. Not as fantasy. As data."

*From "Just Ask" by Pinghsien Wu, Chapter 4*

And the question that cracks David open:

> "If there is no constraint to your ability and resources, what would you do?"
>
> David deflects. Chen: "That's your answer."

*From "Just Ask" by Pinghsien Wu, Chapter 4*

## The Writitation Exercise

### Observe (What is the current state?)

You identified your problem statement in the previous lesson. Now turn outward. Write for fifteen minutes:

> **Describe the person who has the problem I identified. What does their day look like? What have they tried? What keeps failing? What do they complain about to colleagues but never say in meetings?**

Do not write about a demographic. Write about a person. Give them a name if it helps. Describe their frustrations in their own language, not yours.

If you do not know these details, that is the most important thing you have learned today. You need to find out. This is the blank right side of Jenny's napkin -- the side that requires conversation, not imagination.

### Discern (What is the desired state?)

Now imagine that person has found the solution. Not your landing page specifically -- the actual solution to their problem. Write:

> **What does their life look like after? What specific frustration is gone? What can they do now that they could not do before? How do they feel?**

The gap between the "before" and "after" is where your landing page lives.

### Validate with a Question

Write a question you could ask a real person who fits this description:

> **"What is the one thing about [problem area] that frustrates you most, and what have you already tried?"**

The second half matters. "What have you already tried" tells you what solutions exist and why they failed. That is the gap your landing page will fill.

---

## Deliverable

**An audience pain points map** with three columns:

| What They Say | What They Have Tried | What Is Actually Missing |
|--------------|---------------------|------------------------|
| (their words) | (solutions they have used) | (the gap you see) |

Fill in at least three rows. If you can fill them from real conversations, that is best. If you are working from observation and research, note that -- and plan to validate with real people before Phase 04.

---

## Key Takeaway

> "Appreciate what you already have. You have enough. Everyone has enough resources to be successful. The question is whether you can see them."

*From "Just Ask" by Pinghsien Wu, Chapter 4*

Chen's reframe applies to you and to your audience. You have enough expertise to build something valuable. Your audience has enough awareness to recognize their problem -- they just need someone to reflect it back in words they recognize.

Your landing page is that mirror.
