# Lesson: The Translation Gap

**What Nobody Is Filling**

*Novel Reference: "Just Ask" by Pinghsien Wu, Chapter 6*

## Opening Excerpt

> Andreas produced a silver card case from his blazer. Plain, unadorned. He handed David a card.
>
> The card was blank. White. At the bottom, a single line -- a URL in red font. Not any kind of red. Stanford-red. No name. No title. No company. No phone number. No explanation. Just a URL at the bottom of an empty white card.

*From "Just Ask" by Pinghsien Wu, Chapter 6*

Andreas Ramos -- multilingual professor, former Google executive, PhD in philosophy -- embodies a single principle: **clarity beats explanation**. His business card carries nothing but a URL. No title, no credentials, no fifty-word bio. If you are curious enough to visit the URL, you are the right person. If not, no amount of explanation would make you the right person.

---

## The Concept: Finding Your Gap

At the GenAI Summit, David meets Andreas and discovers that the bridge he has been building his entire life -- between Mandarin and English, between technical and business, between Eastern philosophy and Western strategy -- is not a liability. It is his positioning.

Andreas shows David the deep parallel between ancient philosophy and modern AI:

> "Meaning is relational. Beautiful exists because ugly exists. Long is known through short. High through low. You don't define a thing by what it is. You define it by what it's not."

*From "Just Ask" by Pinghsien Wu, Chapter 6*

This is the Tao Te Ching's core teaching -- and it is also how word embeddings work in large language models. King minus male plus female equals Queen. Paris minus France plus Italy equals Rome. Meaning is not absolute. It is defined by relationships.

Your positioning works the same way. You do not define yourself by listing everything you are. You define yourself by identifying the **gap** -- the space between two domains that are not talking to each other, where you stand.

> "The frontier leaders understand both sides. But they're building the technology. They're not translating it for the mid-career professional who just got told to 'figure out AI' by a board that can't define what that means."

*From "Just Ask" by Pinghsien Wu, Chapter 6*

David sees it: "I've been standing in that gap my whole life. Between Mandarin and English. Between engineering and business. Between what systems do and what humans need."

## The Writitation Exercise

### Observe (What is the current state?)

Write for ten minutes:

> **What two worlds do I stand between? Where have I spent my career translating -- between departments, between disciplines, between cultures, between experts and non-experts?**

Think about the moments when you were the person who made two groups understand each other. The times you said, "What they mean is..." and both sides nodded. The bridge you built so naturally you never thought to name it.

### Discern (What is the desired state?)

Now combine everything from Phase 02 into a **positioning statement**. This is the sentence that will anchor your landing page.

Format:

> **"For [specific audience] who [specific struggle], I provide [specific value] by [how you do it differently]. Unlike [existing alternatives], I [your unique positioning]."**

Rules:
- The audience must match your pain points map
- The value must come from your value propositions
- The "unlike" must name what currently exists and fails
- The positioning must describe a gap only you fill

Example: *"For mid-career professionals who have been told to 'figure out AI' but don't know where to start, I provide a structured path from existing expertise to AI-enabled capability. Unlike generic AI courses that teach tools, I translate between what AI can do and what your career actually needs."*

### Validate with a Question

Write the elevator question -- not an elevator pitch. A pitch tells. A question asks.

> **"You know the gap between [Domain A] and [Domain B]? The one where [specific problem happens]? What if someone bridged that?"**

This is the question you would ask in a hallway conversation. If the person's eyes light up, you have found your gap. If they look confused, the gap may not be real -- or you have not articulated it clearly enough.

---

## Deliverable

**A positioning statement** -- one paragraph that defines who you serve, what gap you fill, and how you are different from what already exists.

This statement will become the backbone of your landing page content. It should be:
- Specific enough that someone can immediately tell whether it applies to them
- Clear enough that it needs no explanation (Andreas's rule)
- Honest enough that you believe it

Write it in your incubator journal. Then say it out loud to someone. If you have to explain it, it is not clear enough yet.

---

## Key Takeaway

> "Clarity beats explanation."

*From "Just Ask" by Pinghsien Wu, Chapter 6*

Andreas's white card is the anti-fifty-slide-deck. No performance. No justification. Just a single line of text that says everything by saying almost nothing.

Your positioning statement is your version of that card. It does not need to be clever. It needs to be clear. If the right person reads it and immediately thinks, "This is for me" -- you have found your translation gap.
